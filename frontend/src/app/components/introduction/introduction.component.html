Step 1 of 5  ·  The Problem

Connect cities with minimum roads

A classic optimization problem with real-world impact

Imagine building a road network linking three cities. Your goal: minimize the total road length while keeping every city connected.

This is the Steiner Tree Problem, named after Swiss mathematician Jakob Steiner (1796–1863). It appears in countless engineering domains.

Telecommunications
Minimize cable length in fiber-optic networks connecting data centers
Road Networks
Design highways and pipelines that connect regions at lowest cost
Circuit Design
Wire electronic components with minimal trace length on PCBs
Computational Biology
Reconstruct evolutionary trees (phylogenetics) from DNA sequences
The Steiner Tree Problem is NP-hard in general — no polynomial-time exact algorithm is known for large instances.
Step 2 of 5  ·  Naive Approach

Complete Graph — connect everything

The obvious solution is not the best one

The simplest idea: build a direct road between every pair of cities. This creates a complete graph where each city can reach every other.

For n cities, this requires n(n−1)/2 edges. With 3 cities that's 3 roads — already redundant.

Total length: 772 km Worst
1
With 3 cities: 3 edges, one between each pair — all are kept.
2
With 10 cities: 45 edges — exponential cost growth.
3
Many roads are redundant — you could remove some and still keep all cities connected.
A complete graph with cycles is always suboptimal. We can always remove at least one edge without disconnecting any city.
Step 3 of 5  ·  MST Approach

Minimum Spanning Tree

Remove redundant edges — keep only what's needed

A Spanning Tree connects all n cities using exactly n−1 edges with no cycles. The Minimum version picks the shortest such set.

Classical algorithms solve MST in O(E log V) time — very efficient even for thousands of cities.

Total length: 472 km Better (−39%)
1
Kruskal's algorithm: sort all edges by length, add shortest edge that doesn't create a cycle.
2
Prim's algorithm: grow the tree greedily from any city, always adding the nearest unvisited city.
3
Both give the same optimal result — the MST is unique when all edges have different lengths.

Can we do even better by adding new junction points?

Step 4 of 5  ·  Steiner Tree

Add junction points to save distance

The Steiner point is not a city — it's a calculated junction

The key insight of the Steiner Tree: we can add new intermediate points (called Steiner points) that are not original cities. Roads meet at these junctions, reducing total length.

A Steiner tree on n terminals can have at most n−2 Steiner points. For 3 cities, that means at most 1 extra junction.

Total length: 439 km Optimal (−7%)
Complexity
NP-hard for ≥3 terminals. Approximated within ratio 1.39 (Robins–Zelikovsky)
Savings
Always ≤ MST length. Maximum savings: ~13.4% for equilateral triangle
The purple S point is not a city — it's a virtual junction we introduced to reduce the total road length.
Step 5 of 5  ·  The Fermat Point

The 120° rule for 3 cities

A beautiful geometric property gives us the exact solution

For exactly 3 terminal points, the optimal Steiner point is known as the Fermat–Torricelli point, discovered independently by Pierre de Fermat and Evangelista Torricelli in the 17th century.

Its defining property: every angle between any two roads is exactly 120°. This is the unique point that minimizes the total distance to all three cities.

Mathematical property ∠AFB = ∠BFC = ∠CFA = 120°
1
Build an equilateral triangle on each side of the original triangle.
2
Draw lines from each new outer vertex to the opposite original city.
3
All three lines intersect at the Fermat point — this is our optimal junction.
Our solver uses the Weiszfeld iterative algorithm to find this point numerically — converging to the exact Fermat point in milliseconds.
